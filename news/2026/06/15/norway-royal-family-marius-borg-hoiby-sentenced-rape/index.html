CGN World Brief: Norway’s Royal Family Faces New Crisis After Marius Borg Høiby Is Sentenced for Rape

An Oslo court convicted the crown princess’s son on 34 counts, including two rape charges, and imposed a four-year prison sentence that his defense plans to appeal.

By Helena Price · World · Published At: 15 June 2026 at 10:37 AM EDT · Last Updated At: 15 June 2026 at 10:37 AM EDT

LONDON | Norway’s royal family is confronting another period of intense scrutiny after an Oslo court sentenced Marius Borg Høiby, the eldest son of Crown Princess Mette-Marit, to four years in prison following his conviction on rape, domestic-abuse and other charges.

The verdict

The Oslo District Court found Høiby guilty on 34 of the 40 counts before it, according to reporting by Reuters, The Guardian and other news organizations. The convictions included two counts of rape, domestic abuse involving a former girlfriend, assault-related offenses, drug offenses and violations connected to restraining orders.

The court acquitted him on two other rape charges. That distinction is essential: the judgment did not accept every allegation presented at trial, and the acquittals remain part of the legal outcome alongside the convictions.

Prosecutors had sought a sentence exceeding seven years, while the defense had argued for a substantially shorter term. The court imposed four years and ordered compensation totaling hundreds of thousands of Norwegian kroner to several women. Høiby’s lawyers said he would appeal.

A defendant linked to, but outside, the formal royal household

Høiby is the son of Crown Princess Mette-Marit from a relationship before her marriage to Crown Prince Haakon. He was raised within the royal family but does not hold a royal title and does not perform official duties.

That status has complicated public discussion. The case is a criminal matter involving an individual defendant, not a prosecution of the monarchy. Yet Høiby’s close relationship to the future king and queen has ensured that the proceedings affect public perceptions of the institution.

The royal household declined to comment on the substance of the verdict. That restraint is consistent with avoiding interference in a criminal case, but it leaves the monarchy with limited ability to separate itself publicly from a crisis involving a central family member.

A trial followed across Norway

The trial lasted several weeks and drew sustained media attention. Høiby denied the most serious accusations but acknowledged or partially admitted some lesser conduct. The court’s task was to evaluate each count independently, including testimony, digital evidence and the circumstances surrounding alleged incidents involving women who prosecutors said were unable to consent.

Legal reporting must distinguish between the allegations presented, the findings the court made and the issues that may still be challenged on appeal. A conviction at the district-court level is a formal judgment, but an appeal can lead to review of legal rulings, factual findings or sentence.

The women involved have also faced the burden of a highly publicized proceeding. Lawyers representing victims said the process had been emotionally difficult. Norwegian coverage has connected the case to broader conversations about consent, abuse in intimate relationships and the treatment of complainants in prominent cases.

Consequences for the monarchy

Norway’s monarchy has traditionally benefited from strong public support and a reputation for accessibility and restraint. Recent controversies, including the Høiby case and questions involving other members of the extended royal family, have weakened that protective image.

The verdict does not change the constitutional role of Crown Prince Haakon, but it places the family under pressure at a sensitive moment. Crown Princess Mette-Marit has faced serious health problems, and the institution is already managing a generational transition as King Harald grows older.

Public debate is likely to focus less on whether Høiby is formally royal and more on whether the palace responded appropriately as allegations accumulated. The royal household’s future statements, and any evidence about what officials knew, could shape that discussion.

What happens next

Høiby’s planned appeal means the criminal process is not necessarily finished. The timing and scope of appellate review will determine whether the convictions and sentence remain intact.

The court’s judgment nevertheless establishes a significant legal outcome after months of investigation and trial. The fact that the defendant has royal connections does not reduce the need for precise reporting, nor does it justify treating the case as entertainment.

The essential facts are that Høiby was convicted on most counts, acquitted on others, sentenced to prison and ordered to pay compensation. Everything beyond those findings—including the long-term effect on the monarchy—will develop through the appeal, public reaction and the royal household’s response.

What this means

The verdict is a major criminal judgment involving a person closely connected to Norway’s future king and queen, but the appeal process remains available. Coverage should continue to distinguish formal court findings from allegations that resulted in acquittal.

For Norway’s monarchy, the longer-term question is whether the institution can preserve public trust while respecting the independence of the courts and the privacy and dignity of the women involved.
