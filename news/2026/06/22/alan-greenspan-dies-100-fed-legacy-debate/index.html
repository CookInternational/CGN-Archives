CGN World Brief: Alan Greenspan Dies at 100 as His Fed Legacy Returns to the Center of Economic Debate

The former Federal Reserve chairman helped define the modern central-bank era, but his long record remains contested after the financial crisis that followed his tenure.

By Sophie Keller · World · Published At: 22 June 2026 at 9:08 AM EDT · Last Updated At: 22 June 2026 at 9:08 AM EDT

WASHINGTON | Alan Greenspan, the longtime Federal Reserve chairman whose opaque language and market-moving judgments helped define modern central banking, has died at 100, closing the life of an economist who was celebrated as a stabilizing force before becoming a symbol of the risks that built up before the global financial crisis.

Greenspan led the Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, serving under Presidents Ronald Reagan, George H.W. Bush, Bill Clinton and George W. Bush. His tenure began shortly before the October 1987 stock-market crash and continued through the 1990s expansion, the technology boom, the early-2000s downturn and the housing-market buildup that later devastated the global economy.

His admirers saw him as a central banker who understood how productivity, inflation expectations and financial confidence interacted. During the 1990s, he resisted calls to tighten aggressively when the economy appeared to be growing quickly, arguing in effect that productivity gains could allow stronger growth without immediate inflation. That judgment became a major part of his reputation as a policy “maestro.”

His critics saw something different: a deregulatory worldview that underestimated leverage, complexity and the danger of leaving financial institutions to police themselves. After the 2007-2009 crisis, Greenspan acknowledged that his assumptions about self-regulation had been badly tested. The admission did not end the debate. It deepened it, because Greenspan’s career became a case study in both the power and the limits of elite economic confidence.

Greenspan’s public style also shaped the institution he led. His speeches could be famously dense, and markets often parsed his phrasing as if it were policy code. That style reflected a period when the Fed’s authority rested partly on mystique. Later chairs moved toward clearer forward guidance, press conferences and more explicit communication. In that sense, the Fed after Greenspan became both more transparent and more publicly vulnerable.

The timing of his death is notable because central-bank independence is again under political pressure. Inflation shocks, energy risk, debt costs and conflict over interest rates have made the Fed a central actor in political life. Greenspan’s defenders argue that his long career shows the value of a central bank insulated from day-to-day political demands. His critics argue that insulation without sufficient oversight can leave policymakers too close to financial orthodoxy.

His biography was unusual even before Washington. Greenspan trained as an economist after an early period as a musician, built a consulting career and moved through Republican policy circles before becoming one of the most influential economic officials in the world. He remained active in commentary after leaving the Fed, even as his reputation shifted from near-universal praise to a more divided historical judgment.

The fairest assessment may be that Greenspan’s legacy cannot be reduced to either triumph or failure. He helped steer the economy through shocks and long expansion. He also presided over an era when financial risk migrated into places regulators did not fully understand or restrain. The question he leaves behind is the question every central banker faces: how much confidence is useful, and when does confidence become blindness?

For global readers, Greenspan’s death is more than a U.S. obituary. The central-bank model he embodied spread across economies that now face similar pressures: inflation, debt, political impatience and market sensitivity to every word from monetary authorities. His career remains a reminder that the institutions built to stabilize capitalism can also miss the fault lines beneath it.

What this means

Greenspan’s death returns attention to the policy choices that shaped the pre-2008 financial order and to the ongoing fight over central-bank independence.

His record remains split between the expansion he helped guide and the financial vulnerabilities that became clearer after he left office.

For readers, the practical takeaway is that central banks are not abstract institutions. Their judgments affect jobs, mortgages, retirement savings, inflation and the way governments respond to crisis.
