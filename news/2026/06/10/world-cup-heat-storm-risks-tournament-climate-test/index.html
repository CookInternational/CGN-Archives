World Cup Heat and Storm Risks Turn the Tournament Into a Climate Test

Players and spectators will face heat, humidity and thunderstorm exposure across a 104-match competition in three countries.

By Serena Tao · Environment · Published At: 10 June 2026 at 9:04 AM EDT · Last Updated At: 10 June 2026 at 9:04 AM EDT

MIAMI | The 2026 World Cup begins as a sporting event and a large-scale climate test. The 104-match tournament spans the United States, Mexico and Canada during a season when host cities can experience dangerous heat, humidity, thunderstorms and air-quality problems.

Players generate substantial internal heat during competition. When the air is hot and humid, sweating becomes less effective and core temperature rises.

Wet-bulb globe temperature measures heat, humidity, sun and wind. Sports scientists use it because ordinary temperature does not capture the complete physical load.

Research cited by Reuters suggests climate change has increased the heat risk affecting most tournament matches. The exact conditions will depend on each date and venue.

Miami, Houston, Dallas and Monterrey combine high temperature and humidity. Other cities can experience sudden heat waves even if their average climate is milder.

Mexico City adds altitude, which affects breathing and recovery. Players moving between climates face limited acclimatization time.

FIFA has introduced mandatory three-minute hydration breaks during each half. The breaks apply to all matches rather than only those crossing a threshold.

That consistency helps teams plan, but a fixed break cannot address every condition. Extreme heat may require longer pauses, cooling or postponement.

Teams use cold towels, ice vests, fluids and shaded areas. Medical staff monitor symptoms of heat illness.

Fans face different risks. They may spend hours in transportation lines, security queues and outdoor fan zones before entering the stadium.

Older adults, children and people with medical conditions may be more vulnerable than elite athletes. Water access is therefore a public-safety issue.

Supporter groups criticized restrictions on reusable bottles. FIFA cites security, while critics fear fans will depend on expensive or limited concessions.

Stadium roofs and air conditioning can reduce exposure in some venues. They do not protect travel routes and outdoor gatherings.

Thunderstorms create another challenge. Lightning can require evacuation or delay, while heavy rain can overwhelm transportation.

Forecasts issued weeks in advance cannot determine conditions for a specific match. Operational decisions must rely on current official weather data.

Heat also affects competitive fairness. Teams accustomed to hot climates may adapt differently, while scheduling can place some teams in hotter time slots.

Broadcasters and organizers prefer predictable schedules. Player safety may require flexibility that conflicts with commercial commitments.

Hydration breaks also create advertising opportunities. That financial benefit does not eliminate their safety value, but transparency matters.

Workers face exposure too. Security staff, vendors, drivers and temporary employees may remain outside longer than spectators.

Host cities need cooling centers, water distribution, shaded queues, medical staffing and multilingual warnings.

Climate planning is not a statement that every match will be dangerous. It is preparation for low-probability, high-consequence conditions.

The tournament will provide a global example for future events in warmer climates. Practices adopted now may become standard.

The central question is whether officials treat heat as a predictable operational risk or an inconvenience managed after symptoms appear.

What this means

Fans should review venue rules, official forecasts and transportation plans before leaving for a match. Heat exposure begins before entering the stadium.

Organizers may need to change schedules or procedures when current conditions exceed safety thresholds. Commercial pressure should not override medical judgment.
