Corporate Money Reform Returns as States Test the Post-Citizens United Boundary

Hawaii and Montana are testing new approaches to corporate political spending, reopening a fight shaped by Citizens United.

By Michael Trent · Politics · Published At: 12 May 2026 at 5:10 PM EDT · Last Updated At: 12 May 2026 at 5:10 PM EDT

WASHINGTON | State-level efforts in Hawaii and Montana are bringing corporate political money back into the national debate over elections, speech and democratic accountability.

The Associated Press reported that Hawaii lawmakers sent a bill to the governor that would redefine corporations in a way intended to block election spending.

AP reported that a volunteer group in Montana is gathering signatures for a similar ballot effort called the Montana Plan.

The efforts are designed to test the post-Citizens United framework, which allows unlimited independent political spending by corporations and unions.

Supporters say voters dislike corporate and dark-money influence, while critics argue states cannot sidestep Supreme Court precedent through new definitions.

The legal fight is likely to be as important as the political one. Reformers want a test case, opponents want a swift constitutional challenge and courts may ultimately decide whether the strategy survives.

The stakes are high because political money shapes advertising, candidate narratives, ballot issues and the public’s perception of whether government answers to voters or organized capital.
