Hormuz Pressure Keeps Oil Above $100 as Energy Markets Watch Iran and China

Oil markets are watching Iran tensions, shipping through Hormuz and China’s diplomatic role.

By James Holloway · Energy · Published 15 May 2026 at 6:59 AM EDT

NEW YORK | Oil markets are once again treating the Strait of Hormuz as the world’s most important narrow stretch of water.

Reuters reported that oil prices rose more than 3% after President Trump said he was losing patience with Iran. Brent and U.S. crude both traded above the $100 threshold that can quickly move from market concern to household concern.

The Strait of Hormuz matters because it is a transit point for a major share of global oil and liquefied natural gas flows. When traffic through the strait is disrupted or threatened, traders price in risk even before physical shortages reach consumers.

Trump’s meeting with Xi Jinping added another variable. China is a major oil importer and has a direct interest in keeping maritime energy routes open. Any U.S.-China understanding on Iran would matter for energy diplomacy, but public statements do not yet amount to a durable settlement.

Reports of some tanker movement through Hormuz can calm markets temporarily, but the key issue is sustained reliability. A few ships moving is not the same as full restoration of normal confidence.

High oil prices can transmit through fuel, freight, food, aviation and manufacturing. For households, that can mean gasoline pressure, higher delivery costs and renewed inflation anxiety.

Central banks also watch energy closely. They may look through short-term spikes, but a prolonged shock can change inflation expectations. That is why oil connects directly to bond yields and consumer confidence.

The energy industry faces a different set of pressures. Producers benefit from higher prices, but extreme volatility can complicate investment decisions, shipping insurance, refinery planning and political scrutiny.

UAE pipeline acceleration plans and broader efforts to bypass Hormuz show how governments are trying to reduce chokepoint exposure. Those projects, however, take time and cannot fully replace geography overnight.

The energy story this morning is therefore not only price. It is vulnerability. A conflict, a strait, a summit and a shipping route are all feeding into the same barrel.

What this means

Oil above $100 can affect households through fuel, freight, food and inflation expectations.

The Strait of Hormuz remains a global vulnerability because so much energy moves through a narrow and politically sensitive route.

Readers should watch shipping reliability, not only daily oil-price moves.
