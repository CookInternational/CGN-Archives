Chicago’s Bears Stadium Fight Moves Into a Springfield Clock Watch

Mayor Brandon Johnson is pushing to keep the Bears in Chicago as legislative timing and rival stadium options intensify the pressure.

By Natalie Ward · Local · Published 17 May 2026 at 4:47 PM EDT

CHICAGO | Chicago’s fight to keep the Bears in the city has moved into a Springfield clock watch, with Mayor Brandon Johnson pressing for leverage as rival stadium options in Arlington Heights and Hammond, Indiana continue to shape the team’s path.

Axios Chicago reported that Johnson is intensifying efforts to keep the Bears in Chicago as the Illinois legislative session nears its end. The mayor is seeking more control over the Illinois Sports Facilities Authority, a move that could shift decisions about public stadium funding toward the city rather than the state.

The stadium fight is not only about football. It is about land use, public financing, tax incentives, transportation, neighborhood development, jobs, political credit and the future identity of one of the region’s most valuable sports franchises.

The Bears have pursued a privately controlled stadium and entertainment district, while city officials have argued for keeping the team in Chicago in a publicly owned stadium framework. Axios reported that only Arlington Heights and Hammond remain serious contenders, which increases pressure on Chicago officials who want to show that the city is not out of the running.

The Illinois political piece is complicated. A stadium package can attract support from labor, construction, tourism and business interests, but public financing for sports facilities often faces skepticism from taxpayers and lawmakers who question whether stadium subsidies deliver broad public returns.

Hammond’s role makes this a regional story, not just a Chicago story. A move across the state line would create a major Northwest Indiana development question while shifting some economic benefits, political bragging rights and transportation burdens away from Illinois.

Arlington Heights remains significant because the Bears already moved aggressively toward the former racetrack site and because a suburban stadium-and-entertainment district could give the team more control over parking, adjacent development and game-day revenue.

For Chicago, the issue is urgency. Johnson’s push may help preserve a negotiating lane, but the legislative calendar limits how quickly new authority or incentives can be created. The confirmed story is that Chicago is still fighting. The unresolved question is whether the city is negotiating from strength or trying to slow a decision already moving elsewhere.

Additional Reporting By: Axios Chicago; WBEZ

What this means

For readers in Chicago and Northwest Indiana, the stadium decision could affect transportation, taxes, construction jobs, development patterns and regional identity.

The next watch points are Springfield action, any shift in Sports Facilities Authority control, the Bears’ public statements and whether Hammond or Arlington Heights gains clearer momentum.
