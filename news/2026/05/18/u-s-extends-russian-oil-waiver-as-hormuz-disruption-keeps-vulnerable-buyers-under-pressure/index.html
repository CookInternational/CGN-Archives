U.S. Extends Russian Oil Waiver as Hormuz Disruption Keeps Vulnerable Buyers Under Pressure

The Treasury move gives energy-vulnerable countries more room to buy Russian seaborne oil while Gulf disruption keeps global supply under stress.

By James Holloway · Energy · Published 18 May 2026 at 3:06 PM EDT

HOUSTON | The U.S. Treasury’s decision to extend a sanctions waiver for Russian seaborne oil shows how the Iran war is forcing Washington to balance pressure on Moscow with the energy needs of vulnerable importers.

Reuters reported that Treasury extended the waiver for 30 days to help energy-vulnerable countries access Russian oil amid restricted Gulf supplies and elevated prices tied to the Iran conflict and the Strait of Hormuz disruption.

The move is uncomfortable but practical. Sanctions are meant to pressure Russia over its war in Ukraine. But when another conflict threatens oil supply, rigid enforcement can worsen conditions for poorer countries with less buying power.

India and other major importers have repeatedly become central to the sanctions debate. They need affordable energy, but their purchases can also weaken Western efforts to limit Russian revenue.

Hormuz is the pressure point that changes the calculation. If Gulf supply is less reliable, countries that depend on imported energy must compete harder for available barrels. A waiver can prevent a sanctions policy from deepening a supply shock.

The policy goal is to keep pressure on adversaries without breaking the consumers and governments least able to absorb another oil shock.

Additional Reporting By: Reuters; U.S. Treasury; CGN News Staff

What this means

This matters because the Iran/Hormuz crisis is forcing Washington to make tradeoffs across sanctions policy, oil supply and global affordability.

The waiver shows that energy security can override clean geopolitical messaging when vulnerable countries risk being priced out of fuel.
