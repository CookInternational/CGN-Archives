Brendan Fraser Named Honorary Starter for Sold-Out Indianapolis 500

Indianapolis-born actor Brendan Fraser will return to his hometown to wave the green flag for the 2026 Indianapolis 500, adding a local Hollywood connection to a sold-out race week.

By Rick Ellis · Local · Published 18 May 2026 at 7:34 AM EDT

INDIANAPOLIS | Brendan Fraser is coming home to start one of Indianapolis’ most famous days.

Indianapolis Motor Speedway announced that the Oscar-winning actor and Indianapolis native has been named honorary starter for the 2026 Indianapolis 500. Fraser will wave the green flag from atop the Speedway flag stand before the start of the race, putting a hometown Hollywood face into one of the most recognizable rituals in American sports.

The announcement gives this year’s 500 a local cultural angle that fits the event’s broader identity. The Indianapolis 500 is a race, but it is also a citywide ceremony. It brings together motorsports, Memorial Day weekend, military recognition, celebrity appearances, local pride, neighborhood traditions, downtown hotels, Speedway restaurants, family tailgates and one of the largest single-day crowds in sports.

AP reported that the event has already sold out its 275,000 reserved seats, while general-admission attendance traditionally pushes the total crowd much higher. That matters because the Indy 500’s scale is part of its story. The race is not simply watched. It is inhabited by hundreds of thousands of fans who turn the Speedway into a temporary city.

Fraser’s role is ceremonial, but at Indianapolis Motor Speedway, ceremony carries real weight. The green flag is the visible bridge between the pre-race pageantry and the moment when 33 cars become a live contest. The honorary starter does not decide the race. The honorary starter marks the instant when tradition gives way to speed.

For Indianapolis, Fraser’s selection has an added hometown layer. He was born in Indianapolis before growing up elsewhere, and his return for race day gives the city a chance to fold one of its own into a national broadcast moment. It is the kind of detail local fans notice because the 500 has always balanced global motorsports stature with Indiana identity.

The Speedway has used the honorary starter role to connect the race to broader popular culture. Previous celebrity starters have included actors, athletes, business figures and public personalities. The job is simple in form: wave the flag. But the symbolism is larger. It tells the audience who the Speedway wants standing at the threshold of race day.

Fraser arrives with a career that spans blockbuster adventure films, family favorites and a major late-career awards comeback. He won the Academy Award for Best Actor for “The Whale” and remains widely known for roles in “The Mummy” films. His latest public moment also connects to Memorial Day weekend through the upcoming film “Pressure,” in which he portrays General Dwight D. Eisenhower in a story about the hours leading to the D-Day invasion. IMS has noted the film connection as part of the broader announcement.

That Memorial Day link matters. The Indianapolis 500 has long included patriotic and military elements, from the pre-race ceremonies to the timing of the event itself. A starter connected to a film about World War II planning adds a public-facing theme of service, history and remembrance without changing the central focus of the race.

Still, the strongest local angle is simpler: Indianapolis gets to see one of its native sons on the flag stand. The city does not always dominate national entertainment conversations, but the 500 gives Indianapolis one guaranteed annual stage. Fraser’s selection turns that stage back toward the city’s own biography.

The race itself remains the main event. Drivers and teams are working through the usual Indianapolis balance of speed, setup, traffic, weather, fuel mileage and nerves. The 500 is famous for punishing assumptions. A fast car in one session can become difficult when the wind changes. A clean strategy can be scrambled by caution timing. A favorite can be undone by pit-lane trouble. A long shot can become a contender if the race falls correctly.

That is why the pre-race show is only one layer of the Speedway experience. Fans come for the flyover, the anthem, the celebrities, the traditions and the crowd. But they stay for the uncertainty of 500 miles. The flag starts a race that still has to be earned lap by lap.

The sellout also reinforces the event’s business and civic importance. A full reserved-seat crowd means hotel demand, restaurant traffic, transportation pressure, merchandise sales, sponsor visibility and national attention. Indianapolis builds part of its late-May rhythm around race week. The city’s economy feels the event not only at the track but across downtown, Speedway, airport corridors and hospitality districts.

For local businesses, the 500 is not just a sports weekend. It is planning season. Staffing, inventory, reservations, traffic timing and customer flow all change. Bars and restaurants prepare for visitors. Hotels manage peak demand. Rideshare drivers, parking operators and event workers feel the surge. A sold-out race strengthens that impact.

For fans, Fraser’s honorary-starter role adds a recognizable name to a week already full of rituals. Some fans will care deeply about the racing field. Others will be drawn by the pageantry. Many care about both. The Indy 500 works because it can serve die-hard racing followers and once-a-year fans at the same time.

The choice also fits a broader sports trend: major events increasingly use celebrity moments to reach audiences beyond the core fan base. Formula One, the Super Bowl, the NBA All-Star weekend, college football title games and marquee boxing or UFC events all blend sport with entertainment. The 500 has done this for generations, but the modern media environment makes those moments even more valuable.

Fraser is a smart fit because he brings broad recognition without feeling disconnected from the city. A celebrity with no local tie can still be effective. A celebrity with an Indianapolis birth story gives the announcement more texture. It lets the Speedway sell both star power and hometown pride.

There is also a generational piece. Older fans may know Fraser from earlier films. Younger viewers may know his awards-season comeback. Families may recognize him across different parts of his career. That wide recognition makes him useful for an event that spans grandparents, parents, college students, children and tourists.

For Indianapolis, the headline is bigger than one flag wave. It is another sign that the 500 remains a national cultural property. The race can attract Hollywood names, fill a massive venue, generate national sports coverage and still feel deeply local. Few events manage that combination year after year.

What happens next is the normal build-up toward race day: final preparations, fan arrivals, weather watching, driver storylines, sponsor events and the slow rise of anticipation that ends with the field rolling toward the green flag. Fraser will be part of that moment. Then the race will belong to the drivers.

That is the beauty of the honorary starter role. It is visible for a few seconds, but those seconds sit inside more than a century of ritual. Fraser will wave the green flag, the crowd will rise, the engines will carry across the Speedway, and Indianapolis will again become the center of the racing world.

For a city that treats race day like a civic holiday, bringing an Indianapolis-born actor back to start a sold-out 500 is not just a celebrity booking. It is a homecoming above the yard of bricks.
