Minnesota Prosecutors Charge ICE Officer in Nonfatal Shooting of Venezuelan Man

A Hennepin County case against an ICE officer raises questions about state authority, federal enforcement and accountability during immigration operations.

By Monica Steele · Politics · Published 18 May 2026 at 6:08 PM EDT

MINNEAPOLIS | Minnesota prosecutors have charged an ICE officer in a nonfatal shooting case that is likely to test the line between federal immigration enforcement and state criminal accountability.

AP reported that Hennepin County charged ICE officer Christian Castro with four counts of second-degree assault and one count of falsely reporting a crime in connection with the nonfatal shooting of Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis, a Venezuelan man.

The charges are allegations unless proven in court. Castro, like any criminal defendant, is presumed innocent unless convicted.

The case is politically significant because it comes during an aggressive immigration-enforcement period and because state officials are asserting authority to prosecute an alleged crime by a federal officer.

AP reported that prosecutors said Sosa-Celis posed no threat when Castro fired through a front door, striking him in the leg. The defense and federal authorities may contest parts of that account, and the legal process will determine what evidence is admissible and persuasive.

The New York Times also reported on the case as part of wider scrutiny of immigration enforcement actions. The issue is not only one shooting. It is whether accountability mechanisms are clear when federal agents operate inside local communities.

Law-enforcement accountability cases often turn on video, witness statements, reports, body-camera records, timing and whether force was reasonable under the circumstances.

State-federal tension is likely to grow if the case proceeds. Federal authorities may argue about jurisdiction or officer protections. Local prosecutors will argue that federal status does not place an officer beyond state criminal law.

For immigrant communities, the case will be read through a wider lens of fear, enforcement and trust. For law enforcement, it will be read as a test of operational authority and legal exposure.

The public should let the court process work while insisting on transparency. Charges are not a conviction, but a charging decision in a federal-officer shooting is a major accountability development.

What this means

This matters because the case could test how state prosecutors hold federal immigration officers accountable for alleged conduct during enforcement operations.

The next issue is whether the case stays in state court, what evidence is released and how federal agencies respond.
