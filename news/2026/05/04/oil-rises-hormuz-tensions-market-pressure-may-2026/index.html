Oil Rises as Hormuz Tensions Keep Markets on Edge

U.S.-Iran tensions and uncertainty around Gulf shipping lanes push energy risk back into focus

By James Holloway · Markets · Published 4 May 2026 at 10:30 AM EDT · Updated 9 May 2026 at 4:59 PM EDT

NEW YORK | Oil markets moved higher Monday as traders focused on renewed tension around the Strait of Hormuz, a critical energy chokepoint where disruption risks have kept crude prices volatile and inflation concerns alive.

Reuters reported that oil rose after Iran claimed it had attacked a U.S. warship attempting to enter the Strait of Hormuz, while U.S. Central Command denied the report. The conflicting claims added to an already tense market environment in which traders are watching Gulf shipping routes, diplomatic talks and possible supply disruptions.

Brent crude and U.S. West Texas Intermediate both moved higher as investors priced in the risk that the confrontation could limit oil flows or raise costs for tankers, insurers and shipping companies. Even when physical supply is not immediately disrupted, energy markets often respond quickly to threats involving Hormuz because of the waterway’s central role in global crude and liquefied natural gas shipments.

The Strait of Hormuz connects the Persian Gulf with the Arabian Sea and is widely viewed as one of the most important maritime chokepoints in the world. A large share of global oil shipments passes through the corridor, making the region highly sensitive to military warnings, vessel incidents and diplomatic setbacks.

Recent market pressure has not come from one factor alone. Traders are watching U.S.-Iran negotiations, reports of vessel incidents near Gulf waters, warnings from Iranian officials and U.S. statements about helping commercial ships move through contested areas. Each development can change expectations about supply risk.

OPEC+ has also tried to signal support for market stability. The Associated Press reported that several OPEC+ countries agreed to a modest production increase for June, but analysts remain cautious about how much additional output can offset geopolitical disruption if shipping lanes remain constrained or if insurance and rerouting costs rise.

The economic concern is that higher oil prices can spread quickly through the broader economy. Crude prices influence gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, freight costs, plastics, fertilizer and shipping. A sustained increase can raise expenses for businesses and reduce disposable income for households.

Central banks are watching the same risk from an inflation perspective. Policymakers may look through temporary energy shocks, but persistent oil-price increases can affect inflation expectations and complicate decisions about interest rates. That is especially important at a time when investors are already debating how long major central banks will keep policy restrictive.

For financial markets, the energy story is now tied to stocks, bonds and currencies. Higher oil prices can support energy producers, but they can pressure airlines, transportation companies, retailers and manufacturers. Rising inflation concerns can lift bond yields, which in turn can weigh on growth stocks and other rate-sensitive assets.

The uncertainty is also geopolitical. The market is not only asking whether oil can flow today; it is asking whether diplomacy can prevent a longer confrontation that raises the cost of moving energy through the Gulf. Until those questions become clearer, traders are likely to keep a risk premium in crude prices.

For consumers, the story could eventually become visible at the pump if elevated crude prices persist. For businesses, it may show up first in freight invoices, fuel surcharges and supplier costs. For governments, it is a reminder that energy security and foreign policy remain closely linked.

Oil markets can reverse quickly if tensions ease, but Monday’s move showed how sensitive prices remain to developments around Hormuz. In a market already shaped by war risk, inflation concern and uncertain rate policy, even disputed reports can move prices when they involve one of the world’s most important shipping lanes.

What this means

The Hormuz risk matters because energy shocks can quickly affect inflation, shipping costs, consumer fuel prices and central-bank policy. Even without a confirmed major disruption, uncertainty around the strait can keep oil prices elevated and markets volatile.
