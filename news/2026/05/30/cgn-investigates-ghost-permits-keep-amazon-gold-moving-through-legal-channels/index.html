CGN Investigates: Ghost Permits Keep Amazon Gold Moving Through Legal Channels

Greenpeace says inactive mining permits helped legitimize billions of dollars in gold despite Brazil’s crackdown.

By Monica Steele · Investigations · Published 30 May 2026 at 4:26 PM EDT

RIO DE JANEIRO | A Greenpeace study has put a familiar Amazon problem into a sharper accounting frame: illegal gold can still move through legal channels when paperwork from inactive or false mining areas is used to disguise its origin.

Reuters reported that Greenpeace analyzed 187 permitted forest areas near Indigenous lands and protected areas in the Brazilian Amazon and found that 98 showed no visible mining activity. According to the report, those so-called ghost permits were used to justify the sale of 26.8 metric tons of gold worth an estimated $3.88 billion from 2018 to March 2026.

The finding points to a structural problem in gold enforcement. A raid can shut down a camp. A seizure can remove a shipment. But if the paperwork system still allows gold from protected land to be attached to a permit from somewhere else, enforcement begins after the laundering has already started.

Illegal gold mining is not only an environmental offense. It can involve mercury contamination, land clearing, violence, forced displacement, labor exploitation, organized finance and false documentation. The gold that reaches a buyer may have already passed through enough intermediaries to make the original damage difficult to prove.

Greenpeace’s focus on inactive permits matters because it moves the discussion from visible mines to the legal infrastructure around them. A permit with no mining activity should not produce large quantities of gold. When it does on paper, investigators have a trail to follow: purchase records, transport documents, declarations, buyers, refiners and export channels.

Brazil has increased enforcement under President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva, and Reuters reported that federal police seized a record amount of illegal gold last year. The continued use of ghost permits shows how enforcement pressure can change criminal behavior without ending it. When one route becomes riskier, laundering systems adapt.

The Indigenous impact is central. Protected territories are not empty spaces on a compliance map. They are homelands, water sources, food systems and cultural landscapes. Mercury contamination can move from mining operations into rivers and fish, turning environmental damage into a public-health problem.

The commercial chain deserves scrutiny as well. Gold is valuable because it is portable, tradable and easy to melt into global supply. Buyers may rely on documents that appear legal. But paper legitimacy is not the same as clean origin. The gap between those two ideas is where laundering thrives.

The investigation also shows why satellite and aerial verification matter. Paper records can be checked against imagery and field observation. If a permit area reports production but shows no evidence of mining, regulators have a reason to examine whether the permit is serving as a laundering cover.

Brazil’s mining agency has acknowledged oversight challenges across the vast Amazon region. That is a real logistical problem, but it is not a reason to treat suspicious permits as unavoidable. Risk-based monitoring, stronger buyer liability, digital traceability and rapid suspension authority can reduce the space for ghost paperwork.

The central question is who benefits from the gap between the mine and the market. Illegal miners may extract the gold, but the trade survives only if buyers, document handlers, transporters, financiers or refiners allow suspect material to enter the chain.
