CGN Wire: Greenpeace Says Ghost Permits Keep Illegal Amazon Gold in the Market

The report points to a paperwork route that can disguise gold extracted from protected areas.

By Marina Costa · Environment · Published 30 May 2026 at 4:26 PM EDT

RIO DE JANEIRO | Brazil’s fight against illegal Amazon gold is being tested by a paperwork problem: Greenpeace says so-called ghost permits have helped gold extracted from protected areas move into commerce under the appearance of legality.

Reuters reported that Greenpeace analyzed 187 permitted forest areas near Indigenous lands and protected zones and found 98 without visible mining activity. The organization said permits from inactive areas were used to justify the sale of 26.8 metric tons of gold worth about $3.88 billion from 2018 to March 2026.

The Rio angle is both environmental and economic. Brazil wants to protect the Amazon, strengthen enforcement and maintain credibility in global markets. Illegal gold threatens all three because it converts forest damage into export value.

President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva has pledged to eliminate illegal mining from Indigenous lands and protected areas. Enforcement has increased, but the Greenpeace findings suggest miners and buyers have adapted by exploiting weak points in documentation.

A ghost permit can be powerful because it gives an illegal commodity a legal costume. Once paperwork attaches to gold, downstream buyers may treat it as legitimate. The original location of extraction becomes harder to prove.

The report also raises questions for regulators. If a permitted area shows no mining activity but large production appears in records, that discrepancy should trigger scrutiny. Monitoring has to match documents against satellite imagery, aerial inspection and field reports.

The Amazon’s scale makes enforcement difficult. Vast distances, difficult terrain, river networks and the presence of armed groups can slow investigations. But the scale of the challenge also argues for stronger risk-based systems rather than case-by-case reaction after gold has moved.

The Indigenous dimension cannot be separated from the market issue. Mining in protected territories can bring mercury contamination, violence, disease exposure and disruption to communities that did not consent to extraction.

For international buyers, the warning is direct. A clean invoice is not enough. Gold supply chains need verifiable origin, buyer liability and the ability to suspend suspicious sources quickly.

Brazil has developed tools to trace gold and investigate illegal flows, but criminal networks change behavior when enforcement improves. That is why permit fraud, money flows and export records have to receive the same attention as mining equipment in the forest.
