CGN Wire: Supreme Court Sides With Mississippi Death Row Prisoner in Jury-Bias Case

The Supreme Court ruled for Terry Pitchford in a Mississippi death-penalty case involving Batson protections against racial discrimination in jury selection.

By Michael Trent · Politics · Published 28 May 2026 at 7:08 PM EDT

WASHINGTON | The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 5-4 Thursday for Terry Pitchford, a Black Mississippi death row prisoner whose case raised questions about racial discrimination during jury selection.

The decision in Pitchford v. Cain focused on the constitutional protections created by Batson v. Kentucky, the 1986 precedent that bars prosecutors from using peremptory strikes to exclude jurors because of race. According to the Court’s opinion, prosecutors struck four of five Black prospective jurors during jury selection before Pitchford’s capital murder trial.

The majority held that Mississippi unreasonably applied federal law when it found that Pitchford had waived his opportunity to challenge the prosecutors’ stated race-neutral reasons. The Court said the trial judge failed to conduct the required third step of the Batson process, which gives the defense a meaningful chance to argue that the state’s explanations were pretextual.

The ruling reverses the Fifth Circuit and sends the case back for further proceedings. It does not by itself decide every factual question about the original prosecution, but it makes clear that courts cannot cut off the defense at the point where Batson analysis is supposed to test whether stated reasons hide racial discrimination.

The case also echoes earlier scrutiny of Mississippi prosecutor Doug Evans, whose office was previously criticized by the Supreme Court in the Curtis Flowers case. The Pitchford ruling again places jury selection in Mississippi capital cases under national attention.

For defendants facing the death penalty, jury selection is not a technicality. It determines who hears the case, who weighs credibility and who decides life or death. The Court’s decision reinforces that racial fairness in jury selection is a constitutional requirement, not a courtesy.

Additional Reporting By: U.S. Supreme Court; CBS News; The Guardian

What this means

The ruling matters because Batson protections depend on courts actually completing the process. A defendant cannot meaningfully challenge racial discrimination in jury selection if the court cuts off the chance to show pretext.
