CGN Investigates: Longview Tank Death Toll Raises Accountability Questions

The Longview paper mill disaster is forcing hard questions about industrial safety, worker risk and public oversight.

By Monica Steele · Investigations · Published 28 May 2026 at 6:47 PM EDT

LONGVIEW, Wash. | The Longview paper mill disaster is now more than an industrial accident. It is an accountability test for a company, regulators and a community built around heavy industry.

OPB reported that Washington Gov. Bob Ferguson said the disaster could become the deadliest industrial tragedy in modern Washington state history. A tank at the Nippon Dynawave Packaging Co. paper mill ruptured, releasing a caustic chemical mixture used in papermaking. OPB and AP reported deaths, injuries and missing workers, with recovery complicated by chemical hazards and site instability.

The facts that matter now are basic and urgent. What was inside the tank? When was it last inspected? Were there known signs of corrosion, pressure trouble, structural weakness or maintenance delay? What training did workers receive? What emergency plans existed? How quickly were local agencies and families notified? Those questions do not presume wrongdoing. They are the necessary questions after workers are killed or missing in a preventable-risk environment.

AP reported that the U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board has been involved in examining the broader safety concerns raised by recent chemical tank emergencies. Federal, state and local agencies may each have partial jurisdiction, but fragmented authority can create gaps. A company may answer to one agency on worker safety, another on environmental release and another on emergency response. Families and residents need the full picture, not a maze of separate files.

Longview’s grief is also civic. Industrial employers are often woven into a city’s economy and identity. That can make accountability harder, not easier. Residents may depend on the plant for jobs while also needing honest answers about risk. Workers may value the employer while still deserving safe equipment, clear warnings and serious oversight.

CGN News will be watching for inspection records, environmental findings, company statements, union concerns, recovery updates and official investigative timelines. The public deserves more than condolences after a disaster of this scale. It deserves a clear explanation of what failed and what will change.

Additional Reporting By: OPB; OPB; Associated Press; Associated Press

What this means

The Longview disaster matters because industrial risk is often invisible until something fails. Accountability means identifying not only what happened, but what warning signs, inspections and decisions came before it.
